The January to March 2015 session was the final version of the course, but it remains open until March 2016 so that those interested can register and access all the materials. All assignments are available to try but will not score or be eligible for a statement of accomplishment.

Week 5

In the final week of the AI Planning course we look at plan execution, taking a specific application to deep space mission autonomy. We also give a summary of the common themes found in AI planning methods used in practical applications. We finish up by considering the broader techniques needed both before and after planning to be able to create and use plans intelligently. This includes knowledge engineering for planning domains, time and resource scheduling.

In this final week there are also the Awareness Level and Foundation Level exams to complete. The Awareness Level exam is intended to be straightforward and is designed as a check that you really have met our requirements to be able to offer you the Awareness Level award on the course, which we expect everyone will achieve if you made it to week 5 or have the time to catch up in this final week. The final programming assignment on the course is also still open.

This Week's Feature is on "AI Planning for Space" by Brad Clement from the Jet Propulsion Laboratory, NASA. Hear about the rich history and development of AI planners as used at NASA, ESA and elsewhere, and understand the types of applications of AI planning in a range of past, current and future space missions.

More details of the requirements for all levels of participation on the course are accessible through the link to "Assignments and Exams".

Week 3

In week three we move from state-space search methods to searching in "plan-space" where search space nodes are partial plans. We cover Simple Task Network (STN) planning and begin to address Hierarchical Task Network (HTN) planning, a technique used in most practical planners today.

As this week's features we are very pleased to welcome Dr. David Wilkins - Senior Research Scientist at SRI International's AI Center, the creator of the SIPE-2 Hierarchical Task Network (HTN) planner incorporating a rich set of technical features and which is used in a range of practical applications, and Dr. Alex Champandard of AIGameDev in Austria on AI Planning for Games.

The mid course quizzes this week allow you to check your progress. They do not count for the final score, but we strongly suggest you test your understanding on them, and use them to get a feel for what will come in the actual exams in the final week. If you submit one of the Mid Course Quizzes, you will earn your second course "badge". More details on course badges here.


At the "Performance Level" the first programming assignment and the creative challenge continue to be open.
Fri 23 Jan 2015 1:00 AM PST

Week 2

In week two we describe state-space search, heuristic search methods and the influential STRIPS representation for plans.

As this week's feature, we are delighted to welcome Nils Nilsson - Professor Emeritus at Stanford University, and previously Director of the SRI International AI Center - a pioneer of AI planning who helped create the Shakey robot. His video provides some background on the invention of the A* search algorithm and the influential STRIPS planner.

For those wanting to follow the Awareness Level of the course you should watch the video lectures marked with a ♦ plus the feature video. For the Foundation and Performance levels you need to watch all videos.

For the Performance Level of the course Programming Assignment 1 and a special Creative Challenge are available. Even if your main interest is programming, we suggest you also try to create a digital artifact for the creative challenge. With a good score on the Foundation Level exam and completing the Creative Challenge, we have designed the scoring so you can "just" achieve a Performance Level award without engaging in the programming assignments.

More details of the week 2 materials can be found via the Week-by-Week link in the left column.

Week 1

In week one we give an overview of planning and the approach taken in this course. We set AI planning in the context of task assignment, plan execution and communication with people using plans. We list some of the very wide range of research areas being explored in the field of AI planning.

Each week there will be a "feature" video - this week's topic is "AI Planning for Robots".

For those wanting to follow the Awareness Level of the course, as described in the "Course Information" link in the left hand column, you should watch video lectures marked with a ♦ including the feature video. You can watch them at any time during the whole course, and need not view them all at once this week.

A number of readings on AI planning are suggested as homework, most with on-line PDF copies available, to complement the material covered in the lectures.

We also encourage you to start to use the course social platform including the discussion forums, the course wiki (which will continue to be available beyond the end of the course as a continuing repository of useful information to assist you), share useful information via twitter with hash tag #aiplan, and to get a virtual world avatar and try the (experimental) virtual world meeting spaces to see if they are useful to you. The first virtual world meeting will act as an initial get together and assist those new to virtual worlds.

The discussion forums are already active and have a lot of fascinating information on the interests of MOOC participants. Scan these if you have time or if you have questions, but don't be put off and think you have missed something.

Please take a moment to complete the pre-course "getting to know you" survey so we can get some feedback on your motivation for taking the course and level of knowledge as we begin.

Thanks to those members of the class from the AI planning community who are supporting fellow students by acting as Community TAs.

More details of the week 1 materials can be found via the Week-by-Week link in the left column.

Welcome to AI Planning

 

The course aims to provide a foundation in artificial intelligence techniques for planning, with an overview of the wide spectrum of different problems and approaches, including their underlying theory and their applications.

You can engage with the course at various levels of participation from an awareness level (a few hours a week), through the core foundation level (5-6 hours a week) to the performance level requiring the completion of programming or creative assignments (8 hours a week or more).
